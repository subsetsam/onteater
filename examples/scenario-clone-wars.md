# The Clone Wars, 22–19 BBY: A Manufactured Fragmentation and the War Both Sides Lost

*A scenario for mapping against the Reference Ontology for Galactic Political Economy
(`galactic-economic-ontology.json`). A campaign-level scenario spanning Episodes II–III,
with the procurement prehistory reaching back to 32 BBY.*

---

## The secession: exit as bargaining posture

By 22 BBY, several thousand solar systems had declared their intention to leave the
Republic. The Separatist movement under Count Dooku was, in network terms, a
fragmentation process of unprecedented scale: a coordinated exit of systems from
the galactic legal-commercial order, taking their routes, their output, and their
Senate votes with them. What made the secession viable was not popular sentiment
but its industrial base. At Geonosis, the movement's true membership convened: the
Trade Federation, the Commerce Guild, the InterGalactic Banking Clan, the Techno
Union, and the Corporate Alliance — the galaxy's carriage, retail, clearing,
foundry, and commercial combines, pledging their private armies to the cause the
way other polities pledge taxes. The Techno Union's foundries and the Federation's
droid fleets were not procured by the new confederacy; they *were* the new
confederacy. A polity assembled from firms is a polity whose war aims read like a
term sheet.

The Banking Clan deserves its own line in the ledger. It signed the Separatist
pact at Geonosis while remaining indispensable to Republic finance — extending
conflict credit to both belligerents of the same war. No episode in the corpus
states more plainly that clearing rails outrank flags: exclusion from the Clan's
rails was a threat neither side could afford to make, so neither made it.

## The procurement that preceded the war

The Republic's response capacity did not have to be improvised, because it had
been purchased a decade in advance by someone else. On Kamino, Obi-Wan Kenobi
found a clone army of two hundred thousand units ready, with a million more on the
way — commissioned around 32 BBY, allegedly by a Jedi Master already dead,
without Senate appropriation, without the knowledge of the Jedi Council, from a
sole-source supplier with a decade production lead time. The genome had been
sourced from a bounty hunter for a fee and a clause. It is the corpus's canonical
off-books procurement: the customer discovered the purchase order after the
delivery was ready.

The paper trail had been managed with equal care. Kamino did not appear in the
Jedi Archives; the entry had been erased by someone with archive access, and the
chief librarian's response to the gap — if an item does not appear in the records,
it does not exist — is the corpus's standing warning about inference from
authoritative archives. The war's decisive procurement was invisible in the
official record not because it was unrecorded but because the record had been
edited.

## The vote: emergency centralization

On the Senate floor, the crisis arrived pre-framed. The Military Creation Act had
been under debate for months — the corpus's only extended on-screen argument
about whether to convert fiscal capacity into coercive capacity — when the
discovery of the droid armies mooted the debate. The chamber did not vote for an
army; it voted for a man. The motion to grant Chancellor Palpatine emergency
powers passed with the enthusiasm the moment demanded, softened by his assurance
that he would lay down those powers once the crisis had abated. The powers were
formally temporary and factually terminal: a ratchet dressed as a sunset. Senator
Amidala's verdict from the gallery three years later — so this is how liberty
dies, with thunderous applause — is the corpus's epitaph for institutional
capacity surrendered by procedure.

## The patron on both sides

The war's organizing fact is the one no contemporary observer could see: the same
covert patron sat at the head of both belligerents. As Chancellor, Palpatine
commanded the Republic's clone legions; as Darth Sidious, he directed Dooku, the
Separatist Council, and through them the droid armies. Every escalation was
coordinated with its own countermeasure; every battlefield outcome served the
same portfolio. The stated objectives on both sides — free trade zones for the
guilds, territorial integrity for the Republic — explain individual engagements
and none of the sequence. The inferred objective of the patron explains all of
it: a war whose purpose was the wartime powers it justified, prolonged precisely
as long as centralization required and terminated the week it was complete.

For the commerce guilds, patronage ended the way unsecured lending to one's
sovereign usually ends. Summoned to Mustafar with a promise of reward, the
Separatist Council was liquidated in a conference room — and with it, every claim
its members held. The confederacy's industrial base passed to the new Empire
without compensation: nationalization by lightsaber, the corpus's tersest
expropriation event.

## The settlement: accounts after the war

The Clone Wars ended with the galaxy's fragmentation cost fully realized and both
nominal belligerents dissolved — the Confederacy liquidated at Mustafar, the
Republic reorganized into the first Galactic Empire to sustained applause. Under
identity criteria the ontology insists on, the Republic and the Empire are one
continuant polity: the letterhead changed, the obligations did not, and the
emergency powers were never laid down because the emergency was never allowed to
end. The war's true balance sheet is short. The guilds financed a war that
expropriated them; the Senate voted powers that abolished it; the Jedi
commissioned nothing and inherited an army that would execute them by order
number. The only account that settled in surplus belonged to the patron.

## Analyst's afterword

The Clone Wars resists episode-level coding because it was never a coercion
episode: it was a campaign, a sequence of manufactured episodes serving a single
strategic aim, in which sender and target roles were props rotated by one
principal. The methodological lesson is the one the ontology encodes as its
patron axiom: when one agent bears the patron role on both sides of a conflict,
the visible parties' stated objectives are screened off from the outcome, and
only the patron's portfolio explains the sequence. Contemporary analysts coded a
secession crisis, a procurement scandal, and a constitutional emergency as three
stories. They were one transaction, and it cleared.

# The Naboo Blockade, 32 BBY: A Coercion Episode with a Covert Patron

*A scenario for mapping against the Reference Ontology for Galactic Political Economy
(`galactic-economic-ontology.json`). Worked example A (§15.2). All evidence from Episode I.*

---

## Background: the dispute

The episode begins, as the corpus itself begins, with a tax. The Galactic Senate's
taxation of the trade routes to outlying star systems was in dispute, and the Trade
Federation — a vertically integrated carriage conglomerate holding both a route
franchise and, remarkably, its own seat in the Senate — chose to contest the measure
not on the Senate floor but in orbit. This was an at-risk dispute long before it was
a crisis: a standing disagreement over who may tax which edges of the hyperlane
network, between a legislature with fading enforcement capacity and a firm with a
private navy.

## Imposition: the blockade

The Federation deployed its battleship fleet to encircle the orbital approach to
Naboo, halting all shipping to the planet. As an instrument, the blockade was a
switch rather than a dial: total interdiction of food, medicine, and commerce,
imposed on a member world whose signature the Federation wanted on a treaty
legitimizing the occupation. The Federation's viceroy insisted throughout that the
blockade was perfectly legal — a jurisdiction claim asserted by the sender,
contested by the target, and adjudicated by nobody in time to matter.

Incidence fell where blockade incidence always falls. Within days, Governor Sio
Bibble's dispatch reported that the death toll was catastrophic — a statement about
the population of Naboo, not about its treasury. The sender's own costs ran through
a different channel entirely: every day of blockade was a day of forgone carriage
revenue on the very routes the Federation existed to profit from.

## The institutional response, such as it was

Queen Amidala placed her faith in procedure and was repaid in procedure. The Senate,
receiving her plea, moved to commission a committee. Her assessment of the
alternative venue was equally exact: the courts take even longer. The Republic's
deliberative machinery remained fully intact while its enforcement machinery was
revealed to be null — the decomposition of institutional capacity into separable
components is the analytical heart of this episode.

Her response on the floor was not a concession but an escalation in the political
layer: a vote of no confidence in Chancellor Valorum. The blockade of one small
planet thereby toppled the leader of the galactic government — a leadership
turnover effect landing not on the sender or the target but on the bystander
institution, and, as later films make legible, by design.

## The patron behind the sender

The Federation did not act alone. Throughout the episode its viceroy took
instruction from Darth Sidious, a covert patron whose communications ran end to end
on the deniable channel. The Federation bore the sender role in public and the
proxy role in fact; the patron's objective was never route taxation at all, but the
crisis itself — the vacancy it would create and the sympathy vote that would fill
it. The episode's stated objectives (rescind the tax, sign the treaty) explain none
of the sequence; the inferred objective of the patron explains all of it.

## Defiance, adaptation, termination

Amidala refused the treaty under occupation — "I will sign no treaty" — moving the
episode from imposition toward its endgame with the demanded document unsigned. The
blockade meanwhile leaked at its first serious test: a single vessel ran the
interdicted approach and escaped to Tatooine, repricing the blockade's credibility
galaxy-wide. On the fringe world beyond the Republic's jurisdiction boundary, the
party discovered the boundary's cheapest measurement instrument: a junk dealer who
would not take Republic credits — "Republic credits are no good out here" — and a
local economy where a boy, his freedom, and the parts for a starship could change
hands lawfully in an afternoon's wagering.

The episode did not terminate by negotiation, compliance, or sender withdrawal. It
terminated when a coalition of the surface polity and the co-territorial Gungan
state — two societies sharing one planet and, until that week, no institutions —
retook the capital and destroyed the droid control ship. The measure in force
dissolved with its enforcement apparatus: forcible termination, the pathway that
dominates this corpus and barely appears in the terrestrial reference.

## Analyst's afterword

Every stage of the machine is attested on screen: dispute, threat, imposition,
defiance, adaptation, termination. What the record does not contain is equally
instructive — no Senate resolution ever arrived, no court ever ruled, and the
patron's role appears in no contemporary archive. Any inference from the official
record alone recovers a trade dispute that escalated and failed. The mechanism —
patron, proxy, manufactured crisis — lived entirely in the unrecorded stratum.
